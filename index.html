How it works

You authenticate with the same ed25519 SSH key you may already have. That one key does three jobs: it logs you in, it signs your messages, and — converted to a Curve25519 key — it decrypts them. Before a message leaves your machine, the client picks a random key, locks the message with it once, then seals a copy of that key to each recipient’s public key. The relay sees sealed boxes and nothing else.

  1. pick a random content key K
  2. encrypt the message body once with K
  3. seal K to each recipient’s public key
  4. sign the whole envelope with your ed25519 key

Under the hood: XSalsa20-Poly1305 for the body, SealedBox for the per-recipient key wrap, ed25519 for signatures. Standard primitives, nothing bespoke.

Why Tor

The relay has no address to seize, block, or geolocate, and your connection is hidden from your network. Tor hides where you are; it does not hide that two identities are talking — the relay still sees who messages whom, and when.

Simple on purpose

The client is one small, readable file. No app store, no telemetry, no account system to breach. If you can read Python, you can audit the thing that protects you. Fork it, trim it, or write your own client — barrow is a documented protocol first and an app second.

What barrow is — and isn’t

Holds

  • The relay cannot read message contents — it holds no keys.
  • Messages are authenticated and integrity-protected by the sender’s signature.
  • Anyone can run their own client; the relay can’t force plaintext out of you.

Does not hold — do not assume otherwise

  • You learn recipients’ keys from the relay, so a hostile relay could substitute keys and MITM you. Verify fingerprints out of band. The app can’t do it for you.
  • No forward secrecy: if a long-term key leaks later, stored ciphertext it could open is exposed.
  • Metadata (who, when, to whom, message sizes, who’s online) is visible to the relay. Tor hides your location, not this.
  • The relay enforces envelope shape, not that a client encrypted honestly. Confidentiality assumes every participant runs an honest client.

A solid small-trusted-group system, not a drop-in Signal.
